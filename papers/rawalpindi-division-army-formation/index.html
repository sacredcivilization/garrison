Core Argument

The Pakistan Army's officer corps is not recruited proportionally from the national population. It is drawn disproportionately from one compact zone: the Rawalpindi Division and the adjoining Chaj Doab. This pattern has a documented, entirely secular explanation that predates Partition — British "martial races" recruitment doctrine formalized after 1857, the specific agrarian class the Punjab canal colonies produced, and eight decades of institutional inertia since 1947. This paper establishes that base case before any theological reading is added to it, and before it can be waved away as either coincidence or conspiracy.

I. The Recruitment Pattern — What the Numbers Show

Pakistan's officer corps recruitment has never been population-proportional. Punjab as a whole has long supplied a majority of the Army's rank and file and a majority of its officer corps relative to its share of national population — a pattern inherited directly from the colonial Indian Army rather than invented by the Pakistani state. Within Punjab, the concentration narrows further: the Rawalpindi Division (Rawalpindi, Jhelum, Chakwal, Attock) and the Chaj Doab districts immediately to its southwest (Gujrat, Mandi Bahauddin, and parts of Sargodha) have supplied a share of general officers and Pakistan Military Academy intake that is disproportionate even within Punjab's own disproportionate share.

This is not a new or hidden pattern. Pakistani military sociology has documented it for decades under the term "the recruitment belt" — a corridor running roughly from the Jhelum river to the Salt Range. What is less often asked is why this specific corridor, rather than any other agrarian tract of Punjab.

II. The Colonial Origin — "Martial Races" Doctrine

The answer begins in 1857. After the Indian Rebellion, the British colonial military establishment adopted a formalized "martial races" recruitment theory — the doctrine that certain communities (Punjabi Muslims, Sikhs, Gurkhas, Rajputs of specific sub-castes) were innately suited to military service, while others (particularly Bengali and South Indian populations, associated in colonial thinking with the 1857 uprising's political leadership) were not. The doctrine was racial pseudoscience, but its institutional effects were real and durable: recruitment infrastructure — regimental centers, recruiting depots, veteran-pension networks — was built where the doctrine said soldiers should come from, and that infrastructure then reproduced the recruitment pattern independent of the theory that justified it.

The Rawalpindi-Jhelum-Chakwal-Attock zone was a primary beneficiary of this doctrine. Punjabi Muslim and Punjabi Sikh recruitment from this specific corridor supplied a large share of the colonial Indian Army's Punjab regiments. The infrastructure built to sustain that recruitment — cantonments, recruiting networks, veteran-family expectations of service — survived Partition intact and transferred to the new Pakistan Army with its personnel, its institutional culture, and its recruitment habits largely unchanged.

III. The Agrarian Substrate — Canal Colonies and Landholding Class

Colonial recruitment theory did not operate on an empty landscape. The late-nineteenth and early-twentieth century Punjab canal colonies — irrigation projects that converted the Bar tracts and adjoining zones into settled agricultural land — produced a specific social class in the Rawalpindi-Chaj Doab zone: smallholding and middling agrarian families, land-owning but not landed-aristocratic, with the combination of physical labor capacity, rural discipline, and moderate education that colonial and later Pakistani recruiters sought for officer-candidate intake alongside rank-and-file recruitment.

IV. Why This Matters, and What It Does Not Explain

This geographic-determinism case is deliberately secular and deliberately incomplete. It explains, on documented colonial and agrarian-sociological grounds, why the Army's officer class is disproportionately drawn from this specific zone. It does not, by itself, explain — and does not attempt to explain — why this same zone is also the densest Alid-Sufi shrine geography in South Asia (GP-012), or why the same corridor connects, by transmission chain, to Chisht near Herat (GP-015). Those are separate claims, argued on separate evidence, in separate papers.

What this paper establishes is the floor: the recruitment pattern is real, documented, and has a complete secular explanation that requires no theological premise to accept. Readers who find GP-012's walāya-chain reading unpersuasive can still accept this paper's case in full. Readers who accept both should notice that the same geography carries two independent, non-competing kinds of significance — which is a stronger claim than either paper makes alone, not a weaker one.
