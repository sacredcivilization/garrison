Core Argument

The "duplicity thesis" — Pakistan simultaneously fought and sheltered the Taliban — is the foundational American misreading of the War on Terror. It misidentifies the institutional logic, locates the primary Deobandi-Saudi ideological capture vector in the wrong institution, and fails to distinguish strategic management under constraint from ideological alignment. More critically: it fails to ask whose interests the "duplicity" framing served. The structural outcomes of twenty years of Af-Pak policy — Indian operational infrastructure across four Afghan cities, a Pakistani-targeted Taliban state on Pakistan's western border, twenty years of army engagement on two fronts — are not the outputs of failed policy. They are the outputs of policy pursuing objectives other than the stated ones.

I. The Garrison State — Pakistan's Institutional Logic

Harold Lasswell introduced the "garrison state" concept in 1941 to describe a state in which military specialists become dominant because of the perpetual management of perceived threats. Pakistan's institutional evolution since 1947 fits this category — but with a modification that Lasswell's original formulation did not anticipate: the garrison is not simply a security apparatus. It is the institutional carrier of a founding ideological proposition.

GP-001 (Pakistan: The World's First Ideological Nation) established the argument: Pakistan was founded on a civilizational proposition — Muslim self-determination as the platform for an Islamic renaissance — and not on ethnicity, geography, language, or pre-existing statehood. This proposition requires an institutional carrier, because without an institution with the capacity and the commitment to protect it, the proposition is absorbed and eliminated by the multiple external forces that have strategic interests in its failure.

The Pakistan Army is that institutional carrier. This is not an accident of history or a power grab by military bureaucrats. It is the structural consequence of the founding proposition's requirements:

  • Eastern threat (India): India does not accept the Two-Nation Theory. Its official position requires that Pakistan's founding proposition be wrong — because if a Muslim civilizational identity required separate political expression in 1947, then India's 200 million Muslims are a permanent question mark about the coherence of the Indian secular-pluralist state. India has strategic interests in Pakistan's failure or absorption that are not contingent on any particular government's intentions.
  • Internal capture threat: The same ideological proposition that requires defence from external pressure is also vulnerable to internal capture — to formations that would use Pakistan's Islamic identity as legitimation for merchant-dynasty commercial interests (the Nawaz formation), for Deobandi-Wahhabi theocracy imported through Saudi funding, or for anti-state Kharijite militancy. GP-002 (the Munir Doctrine) documented the judicial capture vector. The Garrison's Series B papers document the civilian political capture vectors.
  • Western pressure: Great power interests in the region — first British, then American, always including the management of Pakistani nuclear capability — create permanent external pressure on the garrison's institutional autonomy.

Understanding Pakistan as a garrison state reframes every question about civil-military relations, democratic accountability, and institutional behavior. The army's persistent role is not the suppression of democracy. It is the protection of the ideological proposition that makes Pakistan's existence coherent — a proposition that elected governments have repeatedly demonstrated they will trade away for external patronage, dynasty preservation, or factional commercial advantage.

II. The War on Terror's Structural Outcomes — Reading the Map

The standard American narrative of the War on Terror (2001–2021) in the Af-Pak theater has two versions: (A) a failed counterterrorism campaign that could not be sustained domestically, or (B) a failed nation-building project that underestimated Afghan social complexity. Both versions share a common assumption: the outcomes were unintended failures.

This assumption collapses under structural analysis of what twenty years of Af-Pak policy actually produced:

When the structural outcomes of a twenty-year policy align consistently with the strategic interests of one power (India) that was not the stated subject of the policy, and consistently against the interests of the stated ally (Pakistan), the "policy failure" explanation requires extraordinary evidence. The structural evidence points in a different direction: the Af-Pak theater was managed, at least in part, as a sustained pressure operation against Pakistani institutional capacity and the Pakistan Army's strategic autonomy.

The India-US Strategic Convergence

The US-India Civil Nuclear Deal (2006–2008), signed at the height of the Musharraf period, is the formal expression of the strategic convergence that shaped the Af-Pak theater. The deal gave India nuclear legitimacy outside the NPT framework while Pakistan remained under sanctions. It created the formal framework for the US-India partnership that made Indian operational infrastructure in Afghanistan a natural extension of joint US-India strategic interests. Understanding the Af-Pak theater without the US-India strategic convergence as its structural context is like analyzing a chess game while ignoring one player's pieces.

III. The "Duplicity" Thesis — What It Gets Wrong

The duplicity thesis — developed primarily by American analysts including Bruce Riedel, Steve Coll, and institutionalized in multiple US Congressional investigations — holds that the ISI maintained strategic relationships with Taliban leadership (the Quetta Shura) while accepting American payments to fight Taliban elements. The thesis implies institutional bad faith at the highest levels of the Pakistan Army.

The thesis makes three analytical errors:

Error 1: Conflating strategic management with ideological alignment. The ISI's maintenance of contact with Quetta Shura leadership was not ideological support for the Taliban's Deobandi-Kharijite programme. It was the retention of intelligence visibility into the most significant political formation in Pakistan's immediate western theater. A military intelligence organization that loses contact with the most powerful political-military actor in the adjacent state is not exercising strategic prudence — it is surrendering the information environment. The question was never "are you for or against the Taliban?" It was "what happens to Pakistan's western flank if CIA and RAW have full control of all contact with every Afghan political formation?"

Error 2: Misidentifying the primary Deobandi-Saudi capture vector. The duplicity thesis locates Pakistan's Deobandi-Wahhabi ideological problem in the army. This is factually wrong. The primary vectors of Saudi ideological capture in Pakistan are civilian-political: the PML-N structural nexus with Saudi financial networks (documented in GP-006), the JUI-F mass constituency built on Deobandi madrassa graduates, the direct Saudi-Pakistani madrassa funding architecture. The Pakistan Army's cultural formation — drawn overwhelmingly from the Rawalpindi Division and Chaj Doab, whose communities are Barelvi-Sufi in religious practice — is structurally different from and in tension with the Deobandi-Wahhabi capture it is now actively fighting.

Error 3: Applying an American analytical framework to a Pakistani institutional logic. American analysis of the "double game" assumes that the correct position for a US partner is to optimize for US outcomes. Pakistani institutional logic optimizes for Pakistani survival. These are not the same objective. When they converge (as they did on many specific al-Qaeda targeting operations), cooperation was genuine. When they diverged (as they did on the question of whether the entire Afghan political space should be handed to CIA-RAW management), Pakistani institutional logic rationally diverged from American preferences. This is not duplicity. It is sovereignty.

IV. Musharraf's Three-Front Management

Reading Musharraf's post-2001 conduct through the "duplicity" frame produces confusion. Reading it through the strategic logic of a garrison state managing maximum external pressure produces clarity. The management operated on three distinct fronts simultaneously:

Front One — Extracting Maximum Resources from the Coalition Relationship

Coalition Support Fund payments, supplemental appropriations, and bilateral economic assistance between 2001 and 2008 totaled approximately $23 billion. This was not incidental to the relationship — it was one of Musharraf's primary objectives in accepting it. Pakistan's economy in 2001 was under nuclear sanctions (from the 1998 Chagai tests), heavily indebted, and fiscally fragile. The War on Terror relationship provided the fiscal architecture for Pakistan's economic stabilization.

The specific uses of these resources: military modernization (F-16 deliveries, helicopter fleet upgrades, advanced communications equipment); debt relief ($1 billion rescheduling in 2001); IMF program support; infrastructure investment through USAID. Musharraf converted geopolitical coercion into material resources — a textbook application of what smaller powers do when confronted with requests from larger powers they cannot simply refuse.

Front Two — Preventing the Weaponization of Deobandi-Afghan Formations Against Pakistani Territory

This is the front that the duplicity thesis misreads most seriously. The question was not whether the ISI should support the Taliban. The question was whether the ISI should surrender all influence over Pashtun-Deobandi political formations in the Afghan theater to CIA and RAW management.

The operational reality in Afghanistan after 2001: India built four consulates in Afghan cities — Kandahar (near the Pakistani border), Jalalabad (60 km from the Khyber Pass), Mazar-e-Sharif, and Herat. The intelligence capacity deployed at these locations, vastly disproportionate to diplomatic requirements for a landlocked state with no land access to Afghanistan, was oriented toward Pakistani territory. The BLA (Balochistan Liberation Army) received financial support, arms, and sanctuary through networks traceable to Afghan-based infrastructure. Pakistani civilian politicians were approached, funded, and cultivated through channels running through Kabul.

In this environment, the ISI's maintenance of contact with Quetta Shura leadership was a counter-intelligence operation — not ideological partnership. The objective was to retain visibility into Afghan political dynamics, provide a channel that prevented the Taliban from being completely converted into a Pakistani-targeted force under CIA-RAW guidance, and maintain the ability to communicate Pakistani red lines to the Afghan political formation most capable of threatening Pakistani territory.

The Counter-Intelligence Logic

Consider the alternative: ISI severs all contact with Quetta Shura. The CIA and RAW now have unchallenged management of every significant Afghan political and military formation. Pakistan's western border becomes a strategic environment in which Pakistani institutional interests have no representation, no visibility, and no influence. The Pakistan Army would then face a western border managed entirely by powers whose strategic convergence is explicitly against Pakistani interests. The "correct" behavior demanded by the duplicity thesis — complete severance of all Taliban contact — would have produced the worst possible strategic outcome for Pakistan.

Front Three — Dismantling Domestic Deobandi Capture Infrastructure

This is the front most systematically ignored by the duplicity thesis — because it contradicts the narrative of army-Taliban ideological alignment. The documented record:

  • January 2002: Musharraf banned Jaish-e-Mohammed, Lashkar-e-Jhangvi, and Sipah-e-Sahaba Pakistan — the primary Deobandi sectarian militias that constituted the civilian Kharijite infrastructure.
  • 2002–2005: Madrassa Reform Ordinance and subsequent legislation — the first sustained institutional challenge to Deobandi madrassa autonomy since Zia's expansion. JUI-F's fierce resistance to these measures confirmed the political cost Musharraf was paying.
  • July 10, 2007: Lal Masjid operation — military storming of the Islamabad mosque complex occupied by Deobandi militants conducting vigilante enforcement. Approximately 100 militants killed including Maulana Abdul Rashid Ghazi. The most direct military confrontation with Deobandi extremist infrastructure in Pakistan's post-1988 history.
  • "Enlightened Moderation" doctrine (2002–2008): The systematic promotion of Pakistan's Barelvi-Sufi heritage as the authentic face of Pakistani Islam — explicitly in competition with Deobandi-Wahhabi authenticity claims. Sufi Advisory Council; Data Darbar visits; Sufi music festivals as state-sponsored national cultural events.

An institution that bans the primary Deobandi sectarian militias, legislates against Deobandi madrassa autonomy, kills the most visible Deobandi militant leader in a direct military operation, and promotes Sufi culture as Pakistan's authentic Islamic identity is not an institution that is ideologically aligned with the Deobandi-Taliban programme. The evidence contradicts the duplicity thesis at its most basic factual level.

V. The "Af-Pak" Construction — A Zone Extension, Not an Analysis

The term "Af-Pak" was popularized by Richard Holbrooke's appointment as US Special Representative for Afghanistan and Pakistan in 2009. It was presented as an analytical acknowledgment of the cross-border nature of the Taliban challenge. It was structurally something different.

"Af-Pak" as a strategic framing accomplished four things simultaneously:

  1. Conceptual extension of the war zone: By coupling Pakistan and Afghanistan as a single theater, the framing made Pakistani territory a legitimate object of American strategic management — not an allied sovereign state with independent interests but a co-equal component of the problem being managed.
  2. Legitimization of drone operations in Pakistani territory: The FATA drone campaign (400+ strikes between 2004 and 2018, peak intensity 2009–2013) was conducted within a sovereignty framework that "Af-Pak" prepared conceptually. If Pakistan is part of the theater rather than a partner alongside it, strikes in Pakistani territory become operational rather than violations of sovereignty.
  3. Inversion of cause and effect: The framing positioned Pakistani army behavior (ISI contacts, cross-border movements) as the cause of Afghan instability rather than as responses to the strategic environment created by 20 years of CIA-RAW operations in the Afghan theater. Pakistan as problem-contributor rather than Pakistan as pressured party.
  4. Pressure justification: "Af-Pak" provided the rhetorical framework for "do more" demands — the sustained American pressure on Pakistani institutional behavior that produced the most intense friction in US-Pakistan relations across the entire period.

The framing was constructed, not discovered. It served specific strategic interests. The analytical question is whose interests those were — and the structural outcomes documented in Section II above provide a consistent answer.

VI. Chaos as Designed Outcome — The Distinction That Matters

The difference between "policy failure" and "designed outcome" is not merely semantic. It carries direct implications for how the current phase of Pakistan's security challenge should be understood and managed.

If the Af-Pak chaos was policy failure — a genuine attempt to stabilize Afghanistan that produced unintended bad outcomes — then the appropriate Pakistani response is patience for the next cycle of American engagement with better policy design.

If the Af-Pak chaos was designed outcome — produced by a policy pursuing strategic pressure on the Pakistan Army while publicly presenting itself as counterterrorism — then the appropriate Pakistani response is the recognition that the pressure is structural, that it will continue through different vehicles regardless of Pakistani compliance, and that institutional integrity is the only sustainable position.

The evidence for designed outcome:

  • Indian operational infrastructure in four Afghan cities, maintained throughout 20 years of US presence, serves no honest counterterrorism purpose but serves obvious purposes in Pakistani-targeted intelligence operations
  • $85 billion in military equipment left for Taliban ensures regional instability regardless of policy direction — a "mistake" of this scale in the world's most expensive military operation requires explanation
  • The TTP's emergence as a specifically Pakistani-targeted formation — the Afghan Taliban had no stated agenda against Pakistani territory in 2001; by 2021 the TTP had conducted 80,000+ Pakistani casualties — represents a fundamental transformation of the threat environment against Pakistani interests that occurred entirely within the US-managed Af-Pak theater
  • CPEC attacks traceable to Balochistan separatist networks with Afghan-based infrastructure represent a structural pattern of attacking Pakistan's most significant strategic economic alternative — not random terrorism but targeted strategic disruption
Musharraf's Own Assessment

Musharraf's memoir In the Line of Fire (2006) documents his September 12, 2001 conversation with Deputy Secretary of State Richard Armitage, who reportedly told the Pakistani Director of Intelligence that Pakistan faced a choice: "be prepared to be bombed. Be prepared to go back to the Stone Age." Musharraf's decision to align with the US coalition was made under explicit threat of military action against Pakistan. The "voluntary" nature of Pakistan's War on Terror partnership requires this context — the partnership was extracted under duress from a state whose nuclear deterrent was being explicitly threatened. The "do more" demands that followed were made against an ally who had been coerced into the relationship at gunpoint.

VII. What Musharraf Preserved — The Institutional Legacy

The Musharraf period's strategic management — imperfect, constrained, ultimately politically unsustainable — preserved three things of institutional significance for the phase that followed:

1. Fiscal capacity. The $23 billion extracted from the coalition relationship rebuilt Pakistani fiscal architecture after the Zia-Nawaz decade of fiscal deterioration and the post-1998 sanctions crisis. This fiscal foundation was the material basis for the subsequent military modernization that made Pakistan's current operational capability possible.

2. The Sufi-institutional inheritance. Musharraf's promotion of Pakistan's Barelvi-Sufi heritage as the state's authentic Islamic identity educated a generation of civil society actors, military officers, and political figures in the alternative to Deobandi-Wahhabi definitions of Pakistani Islam. When the army subsequently articulated its anti-TTP campaign in terms of "Fitna al-Khawarij" — naming the TTP threat in classical Islamic jurisprudential vocabulary as a Kharijite heresy against legitimate authority — it was building on the cultural and institutional ground that Musharraf's Enlightened Moderation had prepared.

3. The demonstration of what neutralization could and could not achieve. Musharraf's period conclusively demonstrated that the Deobandi-Taliban formation could be managed as a non-state actor through selective engagement and pressure. It also demonstrated — through the TTP emergence and the post-Lal Masjid backlash — that once the formation achieved state-level capacity (the Afghan Taliban as Kabul government after 2021), neutralization became operationally impossible. The phase shift from neutralization to direct assertion (Azm-e-Istehkam → Operation Ghazab Lil Haq, February 2026) is the logical institutional response to the condition that Musharraf's period revealed.

VIII. The Current Phase — From Neutralization to Direct Assertion

The Pakistan Army's current operational posture represents the institutional recognition that the neutralization strategy — which Musharraf developed and which subsequent COAS tenures maintained — is no longer viable in the post-2021 environment.

The structural shift: the Afghan Taliban as Kabul government (post-August 2021) is not neutralizable. A non-state actor can be managed through intelligence contacts, financial pressure, and selective military engagement. A state actor controlling territory, customs revenues, and international recognition is a different strategic category. RAW has shifted from using individual Taliban commanders as assets to operating through Taliban state infrastructure — a qualitative change in the vector of Pakistani-targeted operations.

Operation Ghazab Lil Haq ("Wrath for Truth" — the army's own Quranic vocabulary in naming the operation) represents the institutional conclusion: when the threat has passed from manageable non-state actor to hostile-aligned state actor, the instrument shifts from neutralization to direct assertion. The theological self-declaration in the operation's name — deploying haqq/batil vocabulary from the Quranic ontological framework — is the army explicitly stating its own institutional identity as the enforcer of haqq against batil in its own operational theater.

This is not the behavior of a duplicitous institution with ideological alignment to the formation it is fighting. It is the behavior of a garrison whose institutional formation is Barelvi-Sufi-Iqbalian, whose officer class comes from the shrine geography of Pothohar and Chaj Doab, and whose most direct operational description of its enemy — Khawarij, the ancient Islamic term for those who rebel against legitimate authority while claiming Islam — demonstrates institutional self-understanding that twenty years of "duplicity thesis" analysis entirely missed.

The garrison state thesis provides the analytical foundation that twenty years of Af-Pak analysis lacked. Pakistan's institutional behavior is not explicable through the framework of a nation-state managing counterterrorism obligations. It is explicable through the framework of a garrison state protecting a founding ideological proposition against sustained external pressure — pressure from an India-US strategic convergence that used the War on Terror as its operational context, while deploying the "duplicity" framing to delegitimize the garrison's resistance to subordination.

Musharraf did not save Pakistan perfectly. He managed a structurally impossible situation — maximum external pressure, internal Deobandi inheritance from Zia, nuclear deterrent under threat, economy under sanctions — with more strategic sophistication than the duplicity thesis allows. The $23 billion he extracted, the Deobandi infrastructure he dismantled, the Sufi heritage he institutionally promoted, and the ISI contacts he maintained against CIA-RAW management are the four pillars of a record that looks very different once the correct analytical framework — garrison state under external pressure — replaces the framework of a duplicitous partner managing American funds while sheltering American enemies.

The current military operations against the TTP — conducted under the institutional self-designation of a force clearing Kharijite heresy from its own civilizational geography — are the institutional conclusion that Musharraf's period made possible but could not itself complete.
