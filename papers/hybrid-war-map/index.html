Core Argument

Six things Pakistani policy discourse treats as separate problems — Baloch separatism, TTP terrorism, Afghan border instability, international narrative pressure, and domestic media hostility to the establishment — are not six problems. They are six vectors of one hybrid-war architecture. Each vector is individually deniable; together they form a single coordinated campaign against the founding proposition documented in GP-001. This paper maps all six and the connective tissue between them.

I. Why "Hybrid War" and Not "Six Separate Problems"

Hybrid warfare, as a doctrinal category, describes conflict conducted below the threshold of conventional war through a coordinated mixture of proxy military force, information operations, economic pressure, and legal/narrative delegitimisation — each individually deniable, collectively decisive. Pakistan has been the target of exactly this kind of campaign since at least the early 2000s, intensifying sharply after 2021. The reason it is rarely named as such in Pakistani public discourse is itself part of the campaign: Vector 6, below, is precisely the mechanism that prevents the other five from being named as a coordinated whole.

II. Vector 1 — RAW's Direct Operational Support

India's Research and Analysis Wing has maintained, since at least the mid-2000s, direct operational relationships with Baloch separatist networks — financing, safe transit, and in documented instances (the 2016 Kulbhushan Jadhav case being the clearest public evidentiary record) direct intelligence-officer involvement in Balochistan-directed sabotage and insurgent coordination. This is not a claim about routine intelligence rivalry between hostile states; it is a claim about the specific targeting of Balochistan as the province where Pakistan's territorial integrity is most exposed, precisely because Baloch ethnic-nationalist grievance (some of it materially real and administratively unaddressed) offers cover unavailable elsewhere in the country.

III. Vector 2 — BLA as Territorial-Fragmentation Instrument

The Balochistan Liberation Army's operational evolution — from scattered tribal-grievance militancy toward coordinated, technically sophisticated attacks on Chinese CPEC personnel, Pakistani security convoys, and infrastructure — tracks with external sponsorship rather than organic escalation alone. The BLA's targeting priorities (CPEC assets specifically) serve an external strategic interest — disrupting Pakistan-China economic integration — that is not reducible to Baloch grievance politics on its own terms. This vector is downstream of Vector 1: RAW provides the operational infrastructure, BLA provides the deniable local instrument.

IV. Vector 3 — TTP as Post-2021 State-Proxy

Before August 2021, the Tehrik-i-Taliban Pakistan was a stateless insurgent network operating from ungoverned tribal space. After the Afghan Taliban's return to power in Kabul, TTP's status changed categorically: it now operates with sanctuary inside a state, under a government that has declined, across repeated diplomatic engagements, to act against TTP bases on Afghan soil. This is the single most consequential post-2021 change in Pakistan's threat environment. A domestic insurgency problem became a state-hosted proxy-force problem, and Pakistani counter-terrorism policy has been slow to name that distinction, let alone respond to it as what it structurally is.

V. Vector 4 — Af-Pak Zone-Extension

Related to but distinct from Vector 3: the systematic use of Afghan territory as permanent operating space against Pakistan extends beyond TTP specifically to include the broader pattern of the Kabul-Delhi post-2021 convergence documented in GP-013 (Kabul Orientation) — an Indian consular and intelligence presence in Afghanistan that, pre-2021, functioned as operational infrastructure for both Baloch and Pashtun separatist coordination, and that the post-2021 Taliban government's foreign-policy posture toward India has not meaningfully disrupted.

VI. Vector 5 — Information Warfare

Coordinated international narrative operations that frame Pakistan's internal security response — army operations, judicial proceedings, political crises — as evidence of state failure or authoritarian overreach, delinked from the hybrid-war context that produced the response. This vector operates through international media, some Western-funded NGO and human-rights reporting infrastructure (see GP-014), and academic and think-tank output that treats Pakistani state security measures as the primary object of concern rather than the hybrid-war conditions those measures respond to.

VII. Vector 6 — The Domestic Carrier Layer

The sixth vector is the one that makes the other five effective inside Pakistan itself: domestic media formations and political actors who adopt Vector 5's narrative frame — "the establishment is the problem," "the army manufactures the threats it claims to fight," "Balochistan's insurgency is purely a grievance response with no external sponsor" — and circulate it as Pakistani domestic political common sense. This vector does not require conscious foreign coordination to function; it requires only that Vector 5's narrative packages find a receptive domestic audience and amplification infrastructure, which — since roughly 2018 — they consistently have.

Strategic Assessment

No single vector, examined in isolation, looks like war. RAW-Baloch ties are "espionage." BLA attacks are "an insurgency." TTP is "a terrorism problem." Afghan sanctuary is "a diplomatic failure." International reporting is "human rights coverage." Domestic media criticism is "democratic accountability." Each frame is individually plausible and each is individually incomplete. Assembled, the six vectors describe a single campaign whose actual target — per GP-001 — is not Pakistani territory or any single Pakistani government, but the ideological proposition that Pakistan exists to embody.

VIII. What This Map Does Not Claim

This paper does not claim that every grievance underlying these vectors is manufactured. Baloch economic and administrative grievances are substantially real and are not resolved by naming an external sponsor for the militancy that exploits them. Judicial and media criticism of specific state actions is not automatically Vector 6 merely for being critical — GP-002 and GP-010 apply exactly that kind of criticism to the judiciary's own conduct from inside this site's own framework. The map's claim is narrower and more specific: that six things routinely analysed as unconnected are demonstrably coordinated in effect if not always in conscious design, and that treating them as unconnected is itself a strategic advantage for whoever benefits from Pakistan's ideological proposition failing.
