Core Argument

The Pakistan Army's operational orientation toward Afghanistan is not a Cold War doctrine called "strategic depth." It is the Khorasani garrison defending its own geographic formation — the zone running from Herat through Kabul through the Khyber to Pothohar, unified by three independent walāya transmission chains. The Durand Line bisects this zone. It does not bound it. Iqbal addressed both halves as one formation in 1930 and 1938. The Army that recruits from Pothohar — the eastern terminal of this formation — is not projecting power into a foreign country when it orients toward Kabul. It is defending the interior of its own mandate.

I. The Ba'alist Reductionism — "Strategic Depth" as Misframe

In the standard strategic studies literature on Pakistan, the Army's persistent orientation toward Afghanistan is explained through a single doctrine: "strategic depth." The doctrine holds that Pakistan, facing potential Indian encirclement from the east, needs Afghanistan as a strategic buffer — a friendly or controlled western neighbor that prevents Pakistan from being caught in a two-front squeeze. The doctrine is attributed to General Hamid Gul (Director General, ISI 1987–1989) as its intellectual architect and to the Zia-era ISI's support for the Afghan mujahideen as its operational expression.

This framing contains a fact and a misframe. The fact: Pakistan's military planners have used the language of "strategic depth" in their internal and public communications. The misframe: "strategic depth" as the explanatory framework for the Army's Afghan orientation reduces a Khorasani geographic mandate to a Cold War tactical doctrine invented by a specific intelligence chief in a specific geopolitical moment. The reduction is the Ba'alist operation: strip the civilizational content, replace with a cynical ISI instrument, then judge every Army action in Afghanistan by whether it advances the ISI's Cold War agenda.

Once the "strategic depth" frame is accepted, the Army's Afghan operations become permanently suspect — a Machiavellian power game conducted by a security establishment using Afghan proxies for Pakistan's geopolitical interests. The Khorasani mandate disappears. The garrison's defense of its own formation is made invisible. What remains is "the ISI's Afghanistan policy" — a frame that the judiciary, the liberal media, and Pakistan's external critics have used to delegitimize every Army operation from the 1980s mujahideen through the Taliban era to the present Ghazab Lil Haq operation.

III. Iqbal's Two Addresses — The Civilizational Geography That Pre-Dates the ISI

The "strategic depth" doctrine was articulated in the 1980s. Iqbal's Khorasani geographic vision was articulated in 1930 and 1938 — before Pakistan existed, before the ISI existed, before the Cold War existed.

The Allahabad Address (1930) — Drawing the Eastern Khorasani Map

Iqbal's December 29, 1930 Presidential Address to the All-India Muslim League proposed the consolidation of Punjab, NWFP, Sind, and Baluchistan into a single Muslim state. The NWFP — the direct continuation of the Pashtun Khorasani cultural space across what would become the Durand Line — is constitutive of Iqbal's proposed formation, not peripheral to it.

Iqbal's four provinces constitute the subcontinent's eastern half of the Khorasani formation. He is drawing the eastern Khorasani map — not proposing a state with a western border at the Khyber but proposing a formation whose western dimension connects organically to the Afghan-Khorasani zone beyond. The inclusion of NWFP is a civilizational statement: the Khorasani zone does not end at any colonial line. The Muslim state Iqbal proposes is the eastern half of a formation whose western half extends through Afghanistan to Herat.

Khitab ba Jawanan-e-Afghanistan (1938) — Addressing the Western Half

Iqbal's final poetry collection Armughan-i-Hijaz (Gift of the Hijaz, 1938 — posthumously published) contains a direct address to the youth of Afghanistan: Khitab ba Jawanan-e-Afghanistan. This poem is the most explicit statement in Iqbal's entire corpus of the Afghan-subcontinent civilizational unity.

Three features of this poem establish its Khorasani geographic content:

Language: Iqbal wrote in Urdu, Persian, and Punjabi. The Afghan address is in Persian — the shared civilizational tongue of the Khorasani zone from Herat to Lahore. This is not a diplomatic concession to a foreign audience. It is Iqbal writing in the common language of the formation that both he and his Afghan readers inhabit. He is not addressing a foreign nation in its language. He is addressing the other half of his own formation in their shared tongue.

Content: Iqbal charges Afghan youth with the same khudī (self-grounded Muslim identity) he charges subcontinent Muslim youth with throughout his corpus. He addresses them as members of the same Millat — the Islamic community organized by divine principle — not as citizens of a foreign state. The Millat's geographic expression is Khorasani: from the Hindu Kush to the Indus, one formation.

The Shaheen: The Shaheen — the falcon whose mountain habitat is the high terrain of the Khorasani ranges — appears in the Afghan address as it does throughout Iqbal's corpus. The Shaheen's mountain terrain is the Hindu Kush, the Sulaiman ranges, the highland zone that the Durand Line bisects. When Iqbal charges both subcontinent Muslim youth and Afghan youth with the Shaheen identity, he is treating both as inhabitants of the same geographic and spiritual formation. The Shaheen does not know the Durand Line. Neither does Iqbal's Khorasani vision.

Iqbal's two documents — Allahabad 1930 (eastern map) and Armughan-i-Hijaz 1938 (western address) — together constitute a complete Khorasani civilizational geography. The formation they map includes Kabul. It does not treat Kabul as a buffer zone for India policy. It treats Kabul as the interior of the Khorasani zone that the proposed Muslim state is the eastern expression of.

IV. The Durand Line — Bisection, Not Boundary

The Durand Line (1893) bisects the Khorasani formation. It does not constitute a boundary between Pakistan and a foreign zone. This distinction is the geographic key to the Army's Afghan orientation.

A boundary marks the edge of one formation and the beginning of another. The Durand Line does not do this. On both sides of the Line:

  • The same Pashtun tribal genealogies exist — the Afridi, Waziri, Mahsud, Orakzai, Yusufzai communities whose territory the Line bisects have family, tribal council, and shrine connections that the Line cannot interrupt
  • The same Chishti-Qadiri silsila networks run — the transmission chains whose origin is at Chisht (near Herat, across the Line) and whose eastern terminal is at Pakpattan (inside Pakistan) pass through the geographic zone on both sides of the Line without registering it as a civilizational interruption
  • The same prophetic designation applies — the Black Banner Hadith (WP-75) designates the Khorasani army as forming in ancient Khorasan, a geographic zone that spans both sides of the Durand Line

Pakistan's consistent refusal — across every government from Jinnah (1947) to the present — to recognize the Durand Line as a permanent international boundary is not irredentism. It is the institutionalized expression of this geographic reality: the Line bisects the Khorasani formation, and no Pakistani government has been willing to declare that bisection permanent, because doing so would be declaring the Khorasani formation's partition permanent — which Iqbal's civilizational geography explicitly refuses.

Pakistan's Non-Recognition — 78 Years of Consistency

No Pakistani government has formally ratified the Durand Line as a permanent international boundary. This position has survived: Jinnah, Liaquat, Ayub Khan, Yahya Khan, Zulfiqar Ali Bhutto, Zia ul-Haq, Benazir Bhutto, Nawaz Sharif, Pervez Musharraf, and every subsequent government. The consistency across military and civilian governments, democratic and autocratic eras, pro-US and anti-US foreign policy orientations, is the institutional expression of one thing: the refusal to declare the Khorasani formation's bisection permanent. This is not a tactical position that changes with geopolitical circumstances. It is the garrison's institutionalized expression of Iqbal's geographic vision.

V. Ghazab Lil Haq — The Garrison Asserting Its Geographic Mandate

The February 2026 Ghazab Lil Haq (Righteous Fury for the Truth) operation is the most recent expression of the Army's Khorasani garrison mandate in the Afghan-facing geographic zone. The operation's name — Quranic haqq/bāṭil vocabulary — is the Army's own theological self-declaration, using Layer I SCRA ontology in its military communications.

The "strategic depth" framing interprets Ghazab Lil Haq as the Army's latest ISI operation to manage the Afghan-Pakistan border and protect ISI's Taliban assets from becoming too autonomous. This framing is the Ba'alist reductionism applied to the garrison's current operations.

The Khorasani garrison framing reads it differently:

The TTP problem is Khawarij in the garrison's own geographic zone. The Tehrik-e-Taliban Pakistan is not a foreign threat that Pakistan's Army is fighting across an international boundary. It is the Khawarij manifestation — Ba'alist-III formation — that the Zia Glitch (1977–1988) embedded in the Khorasani formation's own territory. The Deobandi-Wahhabi infrastructure that Zia's proxy war built in the Pashtun communities of the eastern transition zone (FATA, KP) is the anti-walāya formation operating inside the garrison's geographic mandate. Ghazab Lil Haq is the garrison clearing Khawarij from its own interior, not projecting power across an international boundary.

The Taliban-state problem is the garrison's western perimeter under threat. Post-2021, the Taliban as a Kabul state — not a non-state actor but a governing power — has become the vehicle for RAW operations using the Afghan state umbrella to run destabilization against Pakistan's western flank. The ISI's earlier neutralization strategy (maintain Taliban contact to prevent CIA/RAW full co-option) worked when the Taliban was a non-state actor. The Taliban as a Kabul state is beyond neutralization. The garrison's response — direct assertion, Ghazab Lil Haq — is the garrison defending its own geographic formation's western perimeter, not "going into Afghanistan" across an international boundary.

The name Ghazab Lil Haq is itself the counter-narrative: the Army is declaring in Quranic vocabulary that its operations in the Khorasani geographic zone are the enforcement of divine truth (haqq) against falsehood (bāṭil). This is not ISI doctrine. This is the garrison's theological self-understanding expressed in its own operational language.
