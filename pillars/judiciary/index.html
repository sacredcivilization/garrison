Pillar II

Judiciary

The Munir Doctrine lineage. 1948 SHC. Judicial Ba'alist capture pattern. How rule-of-law vocabulary is weaponised against state continuity.

Core Argument

Pakistan's judiciary has functioned, at critical junctures, less as a neutral check on the executive than as the vector through which the state's founding ideological identity has been reinterpreted, hollowed, or overridden — beginning with the Munir Doctrine's law-of-necessity reasoning and continuing through a recurring capture pattern in which imported positivist legal vocabulary is deployed against the Objectives Resolution's constitutional theology. This pillar documents that lineage: which rulings did it, on what reasoning, and what institutional infrastructure (domestic and foreign-linked) has sustained the pattern since.

The Munir Doctrine as Origin Point

The lineage this pillar traces begins with Chief Justice Munir's validation of extra-constitutional executive action on grounds of necessity — a piece of reasoning that, once established, supplied a reusable judicial template for setting aside the Objectives Resolution's constitutional constraints whenever the sitting court judged it expedient. GP-002 documents the doctrine's origin and its recurrence across subsequent constitutional crises.

Roman Substrate, Constitutional Theft, and the NGO-Judiciary Circuit

Underneath individual rulings, this pillar argues, sits a deeper structural problem: Pakistan's judiciary operates on a British positivist-law substrate that was never replaced with the ijtihad-based legislative mechanism Iqbal envisioned and the Objectives Resolution presupposed. That gap — documented in GP-010 and GP-011 — is what allows rule-of-law vocabulary, amplified through the foreign-funded NGO and international human-rights circuit documented in GP-014, to be turned against the institutions charged with defending state continuity.
