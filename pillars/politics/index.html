Pillar III

Politics & Establishment

Who has served the ideological proposition and who has not. The Zia deviation. Asim Munir's doctrinal repositioning. The army as only institution with continuous ideological memory.

Core Argument

Pakistan's political history is read on this site not through the usual axis of democracy versus military rule, but through a different question: which actors, in each period, actually served Pakistan's founding ideological proposition, and which used its vocabulary while hollowing it out. On that reading, the army is the one institution that has maintained continuous ideological memory across every civilian and military government since 1947 — including through its own deviations, most notably the Zia period's substitution of pseudo-Islamic performance for the substantive Khorasani mandate this site describes elsewhere. The dynastic political formations documented here are assessed against that same standard.

Dynasties, Networks, and the Capture Period

This pillar's political-history papers do not treat PML-N, PPP, or PTI as interchangeable democratic contestants. Each is read for its specific relationship to the founding proposition — the merchant-dynasty formation and its internal pro-Alid faction (GP-006, GP-016), the dynastic succession question (GP-008), and the 1977–1988 JI-Deobandi network that captured state institutions during the Zia period (GP-005).

The Garrison Mandate

A second strand in this pillar reads the army itself as the institution carrying the Khorasani garrison mandate forward — its western strategic orientation toward Kabul (GP-013) and its role as defender of the walāya-fiqh geographic formation (GP-012) read as continuous with, rather than separate from, its conventional national-defense function.
